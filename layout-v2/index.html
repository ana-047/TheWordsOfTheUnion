The State of the Union: A Time Honored Tradition

On a cold January morning in 1790, against the backdrop of a fledgling nation finding its footing, George Washington personally delivered the first State of the Union address, aware of his constitutional duty and of the precedent he was setting for future presidents.

Thomas Jefferson abandoned the practice as “monarchical” and time-consuming, sending written messages instead.

In 1913, Woodrow Wilson reinstated the practice of delivering the address in person and in 1947, Harry Truman's was the first address to be televised.

The SOTU has since evolved, reflecting the resilience of democratic ideals shaped by leaders throughout history.

How do the echoes of SOTU addresses resonate through time? Can the language of leaders be a catalyst for change?

Similarities and Differences

Historically, State of the Union addresses have varied depending on the context of the time, the issues facing the nation, and the individual speaking.

However, some speeches are more similar than others.

For example, a look at addresses given in the past 30 years suggests that there is a high degree of similarity between speeches given by Democratic Presidents.

Perhaps surprisingly, this similarity also largely extends to speeches given by Republicans (with the exception of speeches given by President Trump).

This is perhaps due to the traditional "Presidential tone" of a speech being generally positive and the themes remaining relatively constant.

The World Stage

One of the objectives of the State of the Union is to address imminent challenges facing the nation.

Adversarial relations, conflicts, and international tensions have all taken the spotlight from time to time.

In recent years, the focus of State of the Union addresses has shifted from prominent mentions of Mexico, a neighbor, and Great Britain, a longstanding ally,

to an emphasis on American adversaries and countries with which the United States has engaged in conflicts.

Over the past decade, China, Afghanistan, Iran, Russia, Syria, Cuba, and North Korea have prominently featured in these addresses, marking a notable shift in the geopolitical landscape addressed by U.S. presidents.
